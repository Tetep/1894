The Lineage

Built by Thomas Slaughter. Held by the Knights. Then the Sisters.

In the early 20th century, fraternal orders were not fringe — they were ordinary American civic infrastructure. Communities organized around them. The Knights and the Sisters were part of that fabric.

After a fire reshaped Liberty Square in the early 1890s, Thomas Slaughter built this second-floor hall in 1894 as a Pythian Castle — the formal name for a Knights of Pythias lodge room. The Knights took possession on opening and held the floor for roughly three decades: ritual work, charters, and the patient civic infrastructure of a fraternal order.

In time the Pythian Sisters — the women's order — took up the hall and held it for decades after. They raised funds for widows and orphans, built local relief networks, and kept the chapter book. The same hardwood floors that hold today's events held weddings, funerals, and installations across generations. The lineage is in the room.

The Stewardship

Kelly's Restoration

[ Kelly portrait — coming ]

Kelly Kinnett walked into the hall and saw what the building had to offer: a working kitchen, beautiful bathrooms, an open floor plan, and the architectural bones of a century of careful use. Castle Hall doesn't need to be reinvented. It needs to be reopened.

The restoration is steady, respectful, and ongoing — the same room, repaired where needed, and held for the next chapter of gatherings, celebrations, and quiet community work.

Castle Hall is a members-only venue.

The hall is reserved for members of the ACA Business Club — a small, intentional community of business owners, makers, and stewards. Member rates for the hall are stunningly low compared to comparable historic venues, by design: the hall is not a rental business. It's a member benefit, run for the community that uses it.
